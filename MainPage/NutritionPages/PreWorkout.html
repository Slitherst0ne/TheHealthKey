What Is a Pre-workout Supplement?

A pre-workout supplement can boost any high-intensity, endurance, or strength-training exercise.

A pre-workout is a supplement that can increase energy levels, improve exercise performance, and stimulate muscle growth. Athletes, particularly runners and weightlifters, use pre-workout supplements before exercises like weightlifting or HIIT (high-intensity interval training).

The ingredients and effects of pre-workout supplements vary by product, but most products use ingredients like caffeine to provide an energy boost or creatine to help increase muscle mass. Athletes typically consume pre-workout supplements as pills or as powders mixed into drinks or soft foods. You can also mix pre-workout supplements with protein powders, which can help stimulate protein synthesis and build muscle. *

Common Ingredients in Pre workout:

  • L-Arginine: Arginine is responsible for regulating the levels of nitric oxide in your body, which causes the blood vessels to relax and improve blood flow.

  • Nitrate:Nitrate salts convert into nitric oxide when ingested, which can improve blood circulation and allow oxygen-rich blood to enter the heart. This helps your body to use oxygen more efficiently during endurance workouts. Nitrates are often labeled or sold as a beetroot extract

  • Creatine monohydrate:Creatine is an organic compound naturally created in the kidneys and liver that is converted into adenosine triphosphate (ATP) during intense exercise. Your body uses ATP as an energy source during high-intensity workouts. Increasing creatine intake can increase the intensity and duration of your workouts, and may improve overall strength and muscle-building potential.

  • L-Citrulline:Citrulline is a non-essential amino acid that helps the body produce more nitric oxide, which improves the body's overall blood flow and may accelerate the body's natural removal of lactic acid, which causes muscle soreness.

  • Î²-alanine:Beta-alanine is a non-essential amino acid very common in pre-workout supplements. Beta-alanine can prevent muscle fatigue during exercise by increasing the body's concentration of carnosine, a naturally occurring compound that helps prevent muscle inflammation and acts as an antioxidant.

  • B vitamins:B vitamins, like Vitamin B3 or Vitamin B12, are common in pre-workouts to help improve the body's metabolism of fats, carbohydrates, and proteins. These vitamins can increase an athlete's amount of energy.

  • BCAA: Branched-chain amino acids include the essential amino acids leucine, valine, and isoleucine. BCAAs do not occur naturally in the body, so athletes consume these amino acids with food and supplements. BCAAs are the building blocks of protein and aid in muscle synthesis, meaning they help to increase muscle mass and provide energy to your muscles during exercise. *

Who Should and Should not take Pre-Workout?

If you are a healthy individual with no underlying health problems, pre-workout is safe for consumption. However, pre-workout may not be suitable for everyone, mainly due to its caffeine content. If you fall into one of the below groups, speak with your doctor before consuming any supplements. Avoid taking pre-workout if you:

  • Are pregnant
  • Have heart disease, including things such as hypertension, arrhythmias, or a fast heart rate
  • Are sensitive to caffeine
  • Have stomach sensitivities
  • Have an underlying health condition *

When is the Best Time to Take Pre-Workout?

As the name suggests, pre-workout should be taken before a workout, and although many people drink it on their way to the gym or during their workout, it should be taken at least 30 to 60 minutes prior to hitting the weights or cardio machines. *

Why should a person consider Pre-Workout Supplements?

Don't believe in the power of pre-workout? Well, a study published in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition reported that combining pre-workout with HIIT workouts resulted in significant increases in VO2 max, training volume and lean body mass while also speeding up the rate at which moderately trained recreational athletes lost body fat. *

NOTE: Pre-workout supplements can raise your heart rate, combining them with strenuous cardiovascular activity can put excess strain on the heart. Other less-serious side effects can include a jittery feeling, increased energy, headaches and nausea. *
